1   What problem are we solving?

Electrical power systems must continuously decide how much power should be generated, where it should be injected, and whether the resulting operating point is feasible within the physical limits of the network. This decision-making task becomes more challenging as renewable generation, power electronic devices, distributed energy resources, and fast variations in demand increase the need for rapid and reliable operation.

A classical starting point is the economic dispatch problem, where the objective is to supply the total demand at minimum generation cost. However, economic dispatch does not explicitly represent the electrical network. It assumes that power can be transported from generators to loads without considering line limits, grid topology, or nodal constraints. In real networks, this assumption may be insufficient because a cheap generator cannot always be fully dispatched if the transmission lines around it are congested.

The Optimal Power Flow (OPF) problem extends economic dispatch by incorporating the physical constraints of the grid. Instead of balancing only total generation and total demand, OPF enforces power balance at each bus and checks whether the power flows, voltage-related variables, generator limits, and branch constraints are respected. In this sense, OPF is one of the central optimization problems in power system operation.

2   Why is fast OPF relevant?

Classical OPF solvers are reliable and accurate, but they may become computationally demanding when the system size increases, when operating conditions change frequently, or when OPF must be solved repeatedly. This is especially relevant in modern power systems, where operators may require fast corrective decisions, near real-time dispatch updates, or repeated evaluations under multiple scenarios.

Learning-based models can provide very fast inference once trained. However, a purely data-driven neural network does not inherently know the physics of the power system. It may predict generation setpoints that look close to the training data but violate nodal power balance, generator limits, branch flow constraints, or voltage angle limits. This motivates hybrid approaches that combine the speed of neural networks with explicit physical guidance.

3   What are we proposing? GLONN

In this paper, we propose GLONN, a Graph-Based Lagrangian Neural Network for DC Optimal Power Flow. GLONN combines two complementary ideas:

1. A Graph Neural Network backbone. The electrical grid is naturally represented as a graph. Buses are nodes, lines are edges, and the topology determines how power can flow through the network. The GNN uses this structure to learn topology-aware representations and predict active power generation setpoints.

2. An Augmented Lagrangian loss. Instead of training the model only to imitate OPF solutions, GLONN adds physical constraint information directly into the training objective. The loss includes the generation cost, equality-constraint residuals, inequality-constraint residuals, and Lagrange multiplier updates. This guides the model toward solutions that are not only accurate, but also more physically consistent.

The key idea is simple: the GNN provides fast topology-aware predictions, while the Lagrangian component penalizes violations of the DC-OPF constraints during training.

4   Main results

The results show that GLONN improves equality-constraint satisfaction compared with the purely data-driven GNN baseline, especially in the limited-training regime considered in the paper. Under normal operating conditions, GLONN reduces the mean nodal power balance error from 0.0219 p.u. to 0.0137 p.u., corresponding to a 37.44% improvement.

At the same time, GLONN retains millisecond-level inference. Under normal operating conditions, GLONN is on average 94 times faster than IPOPT. Under extreme operating conditions, it remains approximately 15 times faster. These results suggest that the proposed architecture is not intended to replace classical solvers in all deterministic OPF settings, but rather to support fast approximate decision-making, feasibility-aware learning, or warm-starting strategies.

The main value of GLONN is therefore not only cost approximation, but the combination of topology-aware learning, explicit physical feasibility guidance, and fast inference.

5   Why does this matter?

Modern power systems require tools that are not only accurate, but also fast, scalable, and physically meaningful. Purely optimization-based methods provide strong mathematical guarantees, but may become computationally demanding in repeated or near real-time settings. Purely data-driven models can be very fast, but may violate physical constraints. GLONN explores an intermediate direction which is a hybrid neural architecture that learns from OPF solutions while being explicitly guided by the physical structure of the problem.
